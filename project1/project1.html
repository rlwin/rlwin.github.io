New York City's Income to Rent Problem

by Rachel Win

New York, NY -— At this point it’s a cliché, the rents are too damn high, but it rings true in almost every major city in the USA. The US bureau of Labor Statistics rents had risen 4.8% since 2021, and for many that can be the unmountable first hurdle towards homeownership. While the issue reaches all corners of the nation, New York City consistently rank amongst the top cities with majority renters, and is notoriously expensive to live in (the cities colloquial saying ‘you can’t leave your house for less than $20’), so we focused our research to the five boroughs of NYC. That and the data sets for the nation would have sent me to an early grave.

We used rental data from Street Easy’s Data Dashboard to extrapolate the average rent of the five boroughs from 2011 to 2020, and compared the percentage increase to that of the US Census Bureau’s Data Sets for the average income across the five boroughs.

The average rent was aggregated and averaged for each year, from 2011 to 2020, a time span limitation due to Census record release, to the show the percent increase of average income over those years within the five boroughs.

When compared to the chart showing a percent change of average income in the five boroughs,

While Manhattan has experienced the lowest rent increase over this time frame, it’s resident’s income average is much higher than any of the other boroughs, however there’s a lot economic growth suggested in Queens and Brooklyn from the rate at which their income averages rose. The Bronx however, the borough with the highest percent of residents receiving public assistance, still lags behind as the lowest average earners for the five areas.

Next, to focus on each borough, the percent average rent change was mapped over the average income change from 2011 to 2020 in each borough to try and identify the areas that might be experiencing the most financial burden from rent hikes.

It's likely that the 2019’s pandemic turned the trend for the average income downwards for most of the boroughs. Except in the Bronx where the income stayed pretty steady, and for the first time since about 2015 income was up more than rent, by percent. The comparative rent to income charts from 2019-2020 are suggestive of mobility and peaks an possible and interesting line of research into the population migration from the five boroughs, as an indication of mobility and financial burden.
