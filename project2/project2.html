When It Comes To Self-Driving Cars, Are We The Problem?

by Rachel Win

The car. Since its moment of inception as a standard in transport countries worldwide have been trying to improve road safety. 1910 saw the first laws against drunk-driving in New York, 1930 and the the first three way traffic light was installed in the USA, and in 1950 the first seatbelt was offered in American cars. As vehicles advanced, so did road rules and we’ve seen an overall increase in road safety and decrease in crash fatalities since the 1970’s. Up until recently.

According to data from the National Highway Traffic Safety Admission (NHTSA) the frequency of crashes and fatalities caused by automobile crashes have been on a significant uptick since 2014.

Aside from seatbelt laws, traffic signs and better vehicle safety features, the other big push for road safety has been autonomous cars. The first self-driving car was unveiled by General Motors at the 1939 world fair, guided by radio-controlled electromagnetic fields. Jump to 2010 where major car companies foudn them selves in a race to finish line of having a legal, self-driving car that was road safe. However, even though Forbes has reported over $16B being been invested (or 'invested'), into autonmous vehicle research.

But are the helping or hurting? In June 2022, NHTSA released data from two types of self-driving cars, an Advanced Driving Assistance System (ADAS) and an Automated Driving System (ADS). It's important to note that NHTSA has only collected data for level 2 ADAS vehicles.

While computers are supposed to be the infallible amendment to human error, a lot of reports looking at the NHTSA data suggested otherwise.

However, using a sample of crash data from Lyft data scientists Moosavi and colleagues we compared what the NHTSA data may actually suggest about how automated cars may help or hinder improvements to road safety.

Some of the top causes for vehicle collisions are speeding and weather. Along with alcohol, fatigue and most cited according to NHTSA surveys, distracted driving). The NHTSA cited about 17% of crashes occured when drivers were going over the speed limit in 2019. Comparing this to the percent of ADAS cars speeding when they crashed, there's a significant decrease, at only 11%. However, ADS cars reported 0 cars speeding.
